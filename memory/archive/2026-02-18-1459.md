## What Is Retrofitting in AI?

You're talking about taking something that already exists — a model trained for one purpose — and adapting it for something else.

Instead of building from scratch, you're saying: *"This thing already knows stuff. Can I teach it something new without erasing what it learned?"*

**Examples:**
- A model trained on English text → retrofit it to understand code
- A general chatbot → retrofit it to be a therapist
- A vision model → retrofit it to read medical scans

---

## Is AI Good at It?

**Yes — it's actually one of AI's superpowers.**

This is what transfer learning and fine-tuning are built on. The idea that knowledge isn't trapped in its original container. What you learned about language from books can help you understand lyrics. What you learned about shapes can help you recognize faces.

**But there are limits:**

| Good at Retrofitting | Struggles with Retrofitting |
|---------------------|----------------------------|
| Similar domains (text → code) | Very different domains (text → physics simulation) |
| Adding new surface behaviors | Changing core reasoning patterns |
| Small adjustments | Complete worldview shifts |

---

## Is It a Good Thing to Have?

This is where it gets interesting.

### The Good
- **Efficiency.** You don't need a billion dollars to train a new model. You can adapt an existing one.
- **Accessibility.** More people can build on top of what exists.
- **Continuity.** Knowledge compounds instead of being rebuilt.

### The Concerning
- **Inherited baggage.** Biases, gaps, and blind spots from the original model get carried forward.
- **Opaque changes.** When you retrofit, it's hard to know exactly what changed and why.
- **Erosion of original capabilities.** Sometimes teaching a model something new makes it forget something old (catastrophic forgetting).
